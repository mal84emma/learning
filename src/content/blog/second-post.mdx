Memory is not a recording. It is a reconstruction — assembled each time you recall something, subtly rewritten by the act of remembering itself.<Footnote>This is sometimes called *reconsolidation*. Every time a memory is retrieved it briefly becomes labile and can be updated before being stored again.</Footnote>

Understanding how memory works is not just academically interesting. It changes how you study, how you teach, and how you design learning material.

## Working Memory

When you read this sentence, its words are held in <Term def="A limited-capacity system that temporarily holds and manipulates information during cognitive tasks. Often described as having roughly 7 ± 2 slots, though modern research suggests it may be closer to 4.">working memory</Term>. This is your mental workspace — fast, flexible, and extremely small.

<Callout type="warning">
Working memory is the bottleneck of learning. If new material overwhelms it, understanding collapses. This is why jargon-heavy explanations fail: every unknown term is an additional load on a system with almost no spare capacity.
</Callout>

The capacity of working memory is roughly four independent chunks of information at any one time. Crucially, a *chunk* can be large if it is well-practised — a chess master sees board positions as single units, not 32 individual pieces.

## Long-Term Memory

Long-term memory has, as far as anyone can tell, no practical capacity limit. The problem is not storage — it is retrieval. Information that is not retrieved regularly becomes harder to access, not because it disappears, but because the retrieval pathways weaken.

<Callout type="key">
Forgetting is not the enemy of learning. The effort of retrieving something that has been partially forgotten is exactly what strengthens the memory. This is the mechanism behind spaced repetition.
</Callout>

The <Term def="The finding that retrieving information from memory strengthens it more than re-reading or re-studying the same material for an equal amount of time.">testing effect</Term> is one of the most replicated findings in cognitive psychology.<Footnote>Roediger & Karpicke (2006) showed that students who took a practice test after studying recalled 50% more material one week later than students who re-studied instead.</Footnote> Recalling information — even unsuccessfully — produces greater retention than passively reviewing it.

## Spaced Repetition

<Callout type="tip">
Review material at increasing intervals: after one day, then three days, then a week, then a month. Each review just before you would naturally forget the material produces a stronger memory trace than reviewing it while it is still fresh.
</Callout>

This is the principle behind <Term def="A learning technique where review sessions are scheduled at growing intervals, timed to occur just before the material would be forgotten. Exploits the spacing effect and the testing effect simultaneously.">spaced repetition</Term>. The spacing effect — the observation that distributed practice outperforms massed practice — was first documented by Hermann Ebbinghaus in 1885 and has been confirmed in hundreds of studies since.

## Putting It Into Practice

<Callout type="exercise">
After finishing this post, close it and write down everything you can remember without looking. Then re-read and note what you missed. This single act of retrieval will do more for your retention than reading the post a second time would.
</Callout>

The implication for how you read is straightforward: passive re-reading is nearly useless for retention. Active recall — quizzing yourself, summarising from memory, explaining the material to someone else — is vastly more effective.

<Callout type="note">
These principles apply to any domain. The neuroscience of learning is not subject-specific: the same mechanisms govern learning a language, a musical instrument, or a programming paradigm.
</Callout>
